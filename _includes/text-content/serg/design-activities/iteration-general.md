<p><span>LENS - Engaging Race and Racism</span><br>
Analyze your game based on acknowledges racism and acknowledges race. How does the world your game is set in view race? Does it affect the characters at all? Do the game systems you create (collecting, gathering, fighting, etc.) insinuate or enforce stereotypes intentionally or unintentionally?</p>

<p><span>LENS - Culturally-Relevant Mechanics</span><br>
Analyze your game mechanics based on meaningful mechanics. If you removed the cultural background from your game, would your mechanics still make sense? Are they inherently tied to the culture you are representing? How might you inject culture into your systems? What invisible cultural values might be embedded that are going unquestioned?</p>

<p><span>LENS - Fine-Tuning</span><br>
Select a Spectrum that you’d like to consider. What could you adjust within your game to change the placement of your game along just one spectrum?
<br>- E.g., If you have a racially homogenous game, what characters can you add or what storylines could you adjust to make the game heterogenous? 
<br>- E.g., If you have a game with low meaningful narrative, could you start by writing one significant subplot that engages a main characters’s cultural heritage directly? What could that “unlock” in terms of story potential and richness of the character?</p>

<p><span>LENS - Engagements with Oppression</span><br>
Where do systems of oppression show up in your game? How are they observed by the player? How are they felt by the player? Is that oppression necessary to the plot? Why? How can unnecessary components be altered or changed to be sensitive to contemporary players? How can the necessary components contribute clearly to the larger mission or narrative of the game?</p>
